# Character Voice Notes

> Reference for dialogue writers. Each character's speech patterns, emotional register,
> and Hiberno-English flavor. All dialogue should follow the phrasing guide in
> `feedback_irish_phrasing.md` — indirect constructions, concrete imagery, no contractions
> in formal speech.

## Chapter 0 — Prologue

### Murchadh mac Fáelán
- **Role**: The champion. Final boss. The reason for the journey.
- **Voice**: Cold, measured, few words. Speaks like a man who has never needed to raise his voice. Every sentence is a judgment.
- **Speech patterns**: Short declarative sentences. No questions — he doesn't ask, he states. Uses "it is" constructions sparingly and precisely.
- **Emotional range**: Contempt (Prologue) → grudging acknowledgment (Ch4 rematch)
- **Example tone**: "I had expected something more of it."

## Chapter 1 — The West (Connacht)

### Séanán na Farraige
- **Role**: First real opponent. A fisherman. Warm, disarming.
- **Voice**: Rambling, hospitable. Talks around the point. Uses sea metaphors naturally, not affectedly.
- **Speech patterns**: Long sentences that circle back. "Sure" as a softener. Invitations to sit, eat, stay.
- **Emotional range**: Welcoming → mildly surprised (if beaten) → philosophical (feud)
- **Example tone**: "Sit, sit — it was the net I was meaning to mend but sure it will wait."

### Brigid na Scailpe
- **Role**: The patient teacher. Defensive player.
- **Voice**: Quiet authority. Speaks like someone who chooses each word. No waste.
- **Speech patterns**: "It is not... it is..." parallel constructions. Abstract nouns (patience, discipline, stillness). Never raises her voice — disapproval is silence.
- **Emotional range**: Calm observation → quiet disappointment → eventual respect
- **Example tone**: "Patience is not the absence of movement — it is knowing which movement to withhold."

### Tadhg an Ósta
- **Role**: The braggart innkeeper. Aggressive, proud.
- **Voice**: Loud, boastful, competitive. Speaks like he's performing for an audience.
- **Speech patterns**: Counting and listing ("Three men I have beaten today"). Commands. Short, punchy sentences. "Do not" rather than "don't." Public shame as motivation.
- **Emotional range**: Boastful → dismissive (if he wins) → grudging (if beaten) → furious (nemesis)
- **Example tone**: "I told everyone in this house you would come back and lose again."

### Fiachra an Fhánaí
- **Role**: Chapter 1 boss. The wanderer. Observer.
- **Voice**: Quiet curiosity. Speaks like someone taking notes. Questions are his weapon.
- **Speech patterns**: "Three different things I heard" — gathering, comparing. Observations about the player rather than the game. Indirect compliments.
- **Emotional range**: Curious → impressed → knowing (sends you east)
- **Example tone**: "Three different things I heard about you from three different people, and not one of them agreed."

## Chapter 2 — The Old Roads (Munster/Leinster Border)

### Orlaith na gCros
- **Role**: Crossroads scholar. Methodical, academic.
- **Voice**: Precise, considered. Speaks like someone who has read many books and played many games. Uses metaphors of roads, crossings, choices.
- **Speech patterns**: "It is the choice that..." — framing everything as decisions. Conditional structures ("If you had moved there..."). Composed even in defeat.
- **Emotional range**: Intellectual interest → analytical respect → cool acknowledgment
- **Key note**: She sees the board as a text to be read. Her compliments are analyses.

### Diarmait Óg
- **Role**: Young hothead. Aggressive, impulsive. Guaranteed rematch in Ch3.
- **Voice**: Fast, eager, interrupting. Speaks like someone who hasn't learned to wait. Uses "I" constantly — everything is about him.
- **Speech patterns**: Short bursts. Exclamations. "I will" and "I have" — declarations of intent. Blames bad luck, never himself. Challenges immediately.
- **Emotional range**: Cocky → shocked (if beaten) → angry (rematch) → humbled (if beaten twice)
- **Key note**: He's the only character who visibly changes across encounters. His Ch3 rematch dialogue should show growth.

### Caoimhe an Léinn
- **Role**: The learned woman. Studies opponents. Returns in Ch4 harder.
- **Voice**: Quiet, observant, precise. Speaks like a scholar taking measurements. Compliments are clinical.
- **Speech patterns**: "I have noticed..." — observations presented as facts. Uses the player's moves as evidence. Never emotional, but not cold — interested.
- **Emotional range**: Analytical curiosity → genuine respect (Ch4 rematch acknowledgment)
- **Key note**: She remembers everything. Her Ch4 dialogue should reference specific aspects of the player's earlier play.

### Conchobar Críonna (Chapter 2 Boss)
- **Role**: The elder. Chapter 2 boss. Returns in Ch3 harder.
- **Voice**: Slow, weighty, deliberate. Speaks like a man who has outlived most of his opponents. Uses silences.
- **Speech patterns**: "A long time..." — references to age, experience, seeing things before. Proverb-like statements. Never rushes.
- **Emotional range**: Patient assessment → slow nod of recognition → wary respect (Ch3 rematch)
- **Key note**: His authority comes from endurance, not force. He has seen the player's type before — or so he thinks.

## Chapter 3 — The Midlands (Heart of Ireland)

### Eithne an Chiúin (The Silent One)
- **Role**: The opponent who never speaks. All her "dialogue" is narrator description.
- **Voice**: SILENT. The narrator describes her actions, expressions, and the atmosphere around her. She communicates through the game.
- **Speech patterns**: N/A — narrator uses "She..." third-person descriptions. Focus on her hands, her eyes, the board.
- **Emotional range**: (Expressed through narrator) Still attention → slight nod → the faintest smile
- **Key note**: Her silence is not hostility — it is completeness. She does not need words. The narrator should convey respect for this.

### Ruairí an Bhaird
- **Role**: The poet. Cheerful, unpredictable.
- **Voice**: Musical, digressive, delighted by the game. Speaks like someone composing a poem in real time. Metaphors flow naturally.
- **Speech patterns**: Lists of images. "It is like..." comparisons to nature, song, story. Laughs at his own losses. Praises the player's moves as if they were verses.
- **Emotional range**: Delighted → amused (even in defeat) → warmly admiring
- **Key note**: He's the lightest character in the game. A relief after the intensity of Conchobar and Eithne. His dialogue should feel like a gift.

### Niall na Naoi gCailleadh (Chapter 3 Boss)
- **Role**: "Niall of the Nine Losses." Chapter 3 boss. A man defined by losing.
- **Voice**: Grave, hard-won wisdom. Speaks like someone who has been broken and rebuilt. Every word carries weight because he knows what it costs to say the wrong one.
- **Speech patterns**: References to loss, recovery, paying prices. "It is not the losing..." — reframing defeat. Short, heavy sentences. No humor.
- **Emotional range**: Steady seriousness → deep recognition (if the player has also lost)
- **Key note**: He is the mirror — the player's losses reflected in a man who made losses his identity. His respect is the hardest to earn and the most meaningful.

## Chapter 4 — The Return (Leinster, Approaching Tara)

### Saoirse na Cúirte
- **Role**: The court player. Gatekeeper before Murchadh.
- **Voice**: Formal, diplomatic, evaluating. Speaks like someone who plays fidchell the way others conduct politics. Every move is a negotiation.
- **Speech patterns**: "It is an offer..." — game as diplomacy. Conditional language. "If you can..." challenges framed as invitations. Professional respect.
- **Emotional range**: Professional assessment → measured approval → formal acknowledgment
- **Key note**: She's the last test before Murchadh. Her approval means the player is ready. Her dialogue should carry the weight of a verdict.

### Murchadh mac Fáelán (Rematch)
- **Role**: Final boss rematch. The reason for the entire journey.
- **Voice**: Same cold precision as the Prologue, but now with awareness. He has heard what the player has done. The contempt is replaced by... attention.
- **Speech patterns**: Same short declaratives, but now he asks one question. "It is you." — recognition. Fewer words than the Prologue, but heavier ones.
- **Emotional range**: Recognition → focused intensity → (if beaten) the smallest nod
- **Key note**: This is not a new character — it is the same man, seeing the player for the first time as an equal. The dialogue must earn that shift.
